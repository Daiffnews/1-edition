Description

Rarely has a state so fully changed its character in such a short period of time. Previously content with playing a role suitable to its small scale, up until the early 1990s Qatar was a conservative, risk-averse Gulf monarchy.

 

In 1995, a bloodless coup put an emerging elite to power with a radical dream for the future. Financed by gas exports and secured by a US security shield, Qatar diversified its foreign ties to include Iran and Israel, set up satellite broadcaster Al Jazeera, took a leading role in international diplomacy, and hosted a variety of top-level sporting tournaments, culminating in the successful 2022 FIFA World Cup bid.

 

The diverse, sometimes confused, Qatar policies converge to spread a distinct brand. Either to combat regional economic rivals or to tie Qatar together with the economies of the world’s leading countries, this brand is innovatively designed to address a variety of security concerns; in short, Qatar is diversifying its dependencies.

 

Qatar’s prominent position in the Arab Spring follows a similar trend, but the gamble it plays in supporting Islamists and ousting dictators is potentially dangerous: it is not only at risk of ‘blowback’ in engaging with these actors, but a lack of accountability means that clichés and stereotypes threaten to undermine ‘Qatar brand.’
